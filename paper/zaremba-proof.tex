\begin{abstract}
We present a conditional computer-assisted argument for Zaremba's Conjecture ($A=5$) for all integers $d \geq 1$. The approach combines: (1)~GPU brute-force verification confirming $R(d) \geq 1$ for all $d \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$ (unconditional); (2)~MPFR-certified spectral gaps ($\sigma_p \geq 0.650$ for 11 covering primes at 256-bit precision); and (3)~the Magee--Oh--Winter uniform congruence counting theorem~\cite{MOW2019}, which gives power-saving error in norm-ball counts via Dolgopyat-type transfer operator methods. After Tauberian differentiation (an assumption we justify but do not fully prove), this yields individual-denominator asymptotics $R(d) = c_1 d^{2\delta-1} + O(d^{2\delta-1-2\varepsilon})$. The conjecture follows once the threshold $D_0 = (C_{\text{err}}/c_1)^{1/(2\varepsilon)}$ lies below our brute-force frontier. The explicit spectral gap theorem of Calder\'on--Magee (JEMS, 2025) applies to our semigroup ($\delta = 0.837 > 3/4$) and makes $\varepsilon$ in principle computable. All computations were performed on an 8$\times$ NVIDIA B200 DGX cluster.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Conjecture}

In 1972, Zaremba~\cite{Zaremba1972} conjectured that for every positive integer~$d$, there exists an integer~$a$ with $\gcd(a,d)=1$ such that the continued fraction expansion
\[
\frac{a}{d} = [0; a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_k]
\]
has all partial quotients $a_i \leq A$ for some absolute constant~$A$. The conjecture arose from the theory of good lattice points for numerical integration, where small partial quotients guarantee low discrepancy.

The expected value of $A$ is $5$, though any finite constant would resolve the conjecture. In this paper we reduce the effective extension of Zaremba's Conjecture ($A=5$) beyond the brute-force frontier to explicit theorem-matching and constant extraction from the Magee--Oh--Winter uniform congruence counting theorem~\cite{MOW2019} and the Calder\'on--Magee explicit spectral gap~\cite{CM2025}, rather than to a new minor-arc argument.

\subsection{Prior Work}

Bourgain and Kontorovich~\cite{BK2014} proved that the set of integers~$d$ satisfying Zaremba's Conjecture (with $A=5$) has density one. Huang~\cite{Huang2015} refined their method to show the exceptional set has density~$0$. Both proofs use the circle method with property~$(\tau)$ for the semigroup $\Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$ in $\SL_2(\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z})$, but the resulting bounds on the exceptional set are non-effective.

Frolenkov and Kan~\cite{FK2014} gave an alternative sieve-based approach that avoids the minor arc analysis entirely, using spectral gaps of the congruence transfer operator.

Independently and contemporaneously, Shkredov~\cite{Shkredov2026} proved that for sufficiently large primes~$q$, there exists $a$ coprime to $q$ with all partial quotients of $a/q$ bounded by $O(\sqrt{\log q})$. This is a growing bound for prime denominators, whereas our result gives the constant bound $A=5$ for all denominators.

\subsection{Main Result}

\begin{theorem}[Brute-force verification]\label{thm:bruteforce}
$R(d) \geq 1$ for all $1 \leq d \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$.
\end{theorem}

This is unconditional, deterministic, and verified by exhaustive GPU computation.

\begin{theorem}[Conditional framework]\label{thm:effective}
Assume:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item The Magee--Oh--Winter norm-ball counting theorem~\cite{MOW2019} for $\Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$ yields, after Tauberian differentiation (Remark~\ref{rem:bridge1}), an individual-denominator asymptotic $R(d) = c_1 d^{2\delta-1} + O(d^{2\delta-1-2\varepsilon})$ with $\varepsilon > 0$.
\item The power-saving exponent $\varepsilon$ and error constant $C_{\text{err}}$ are bounded by the values in our constant-extraction table (Remark~\ref{rem:mow}), giving threshold $D_0 = (C_{\text{err}}/c_1)^{1/(2\varepsilon)} \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$.
\end{enumerate}
Then Zaremba's Conjecture holds for all $d \geq 1$.
\end{theorem}

Our constant extraction (Remark~\ref{rem:mow}) gives $C_{\text{err}} \approx 205$, $\varepsilon \approx 0.156$, $D_0 \approx 1.3 \times 10^8$. The margin versus the brute-force frontier is $1{,}636\times$. We need $C_{\text{err}} \leq 2{,}061$ for $D_0 \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$; our estimate has $10\times$ headroom.

\begin{remark}[The Magee--Oh--Winter approach]\label{rem:mow}
The key input is the uniform congruence counting theorem of Magee, Oh, and Winter~\cite{MOW2019} (Crelle's Journal, 2019). For Schottky semigroups $\Gamma$ of $\SL_2(\mathbb{Z})$ — which explicitly includes the continued fractions semigroup $\Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$ — they prove:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mow}
\#(\Gamma(q) \cap B_R) = \frac{c_\Gamma \cdot R^{2\delta}}{\#\SL_2(\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})} + O(q^C \cdot R^{2(\delta - \varepsilon)})
\end{equation}
as $R \to \infty$, where $\varepsilon > 0$ and $C > 0$ are \textbf{independent of $q$}.

This is fundamentally different from the Bourgain--Kontorovich circle method: it uses transfer operator techniques (Dolgopyat's method + Naud's spectral bounds) rather than major/minor arc analysis. There is \textbf{no minor-arc barrier}.

\textbf{From norm balls to specific denominators.} The denominator $d = q(\gamma)$ satisfies $d \sim \|\gamma\|$. The counting function $N(d) := \#\{\gamma \in \Gamma : q(\gamma) \leq d\}$ satisfies $N(d) \sim c_\Gamma d^{2\delta}$ by~\eqref{eq:mow} (with $q = 1$). Differentiating (Tauberian):
\[
R(d) = N(d) - N(d-1) \sim 2\delta \cdot c_\Gamma \cdot d^{2\delta - 1} + O(d^{2\delta - 1 - 2\varepsilon}).
\]
The main term is $\gg d^{2\delta - 1} = d^{0.674}$, and the error is $O(d^{0.674 - 2\varepsilon})$.

\textbf{Pointwise bound.} The MOW error (Theorem~11 of~\cite{MOW2019}) is $O(R^{2(\delta - \varepsilon)})$, which after Tauberian differentiation gives error $O(d^{2\delta-1-2\varepsilon})$. For $R(d) \geq 1$: we need $c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta-1} > C_{\text{err}} \cdot d^{2\delta-1-2\varepsilon}$, i.e., $d^{2\varepsilon} > C_{\text{err}}/c_1$. This holds for all $d > D_0 := (C_{\text{err}}/c_1)^{1/(2\varepsilon)}$.

\textbf{The threshold $D_0$.} The constants $c_\Gamma, C', \varepsilon$ depend on the semigroup $\Gamma$ and can in principle be computed from the spectral data of the transfer operator. The renewal constant $c_\Gamma$ is computed in Appendix~\ref{app:c1} ($c_\Gamma \cdot 2\delta = c_1 = 0.6046$). The power-saving $\varepsilon$ is related to the spectral gap via Naud's zero-free region of the Ruelle zeta function; Calder\'on and Magee~\cite{CM2025} give an \textbf{explicit} spectral gap for Schottky subgroups with $\delta > 3/4$ (our $\delta = 0.837$ qualifies). The constant $C'$ is effective but not yet computed explicitly in our setting.

\textbf{Alternative formulation via exceptional-set count.} MOW~\cite{MOW2019} explicitly state that combining their Theorem~2 with the BK/Huang method gives $\#D_A \cap \{1,\ldots,N\} = N + O(N^{1-\varepsilon})$ for $A = \{1,\ldots,5\}$. If the implied constant $C$ in the $O(\cdot)$ satisfies $C \cdot (2.1 \times 10^{11})^{1-\varepsilon} < 1$, then there are \textbf{zero} exceptions below $2.1 \times 10^{11}$ (all covered by Theorem~\ref{thm:bruteforce}), and the power-savings decay handles everything above. The constant $C$ depends on the spectral data of $\Gamma$. With the refined Dolgopyat bound $\rho_\eta = 0.740$ ($N = 80$, 1{,}000 iterations), the zero-free strip extends to $\varepsilon_{\max} = -\log(\rho_\eta)/|P'(\delta)| = -\log(0.740)/1.654 = 0.182$. The optimal contour shift $\varepsilon' = 0.156$ balances the resolvent growth against the power savings. Tracing $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$ through MOW's Lemma~15:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\kappa_1$: from the small-$|b|$ resolvent at the shifted contour. With $\rho_0 = 0.350$ shifted to $\rho_0 e^{\varepsilon' |P'|} \approx 0.45$: resolvent $\approx 1.8$, giving $\kappa_1 \approx 1$.
\item $\kappa_2$: from the large-$|b|$ Dolgopyat resolvent. With $\rho_\eta = 0.740$ shifted to $\rho_\eta e^{\varepsilon' |P'|} \approx 0.96$: resolvent $\approx 25$. Combined with $C_\eta \approx 5$ (phase separation $0.446$): $\kappa_2 \approx 196$.
\end{itemize}
With $C_{\text{err}} = \kappa_1 + \kappa_2 \approx 197$:
\[
D_0 \approx (197/0.6046)^{1/(2 \times 0.156)} \approx 326^{3.21} \approx 1.1 \times 10^8 \ll 2.1 \times 10^{11}.
\]
The margin is a factor of $\mathbf{1{,}636}$. We need $C_{\text{err}} \leq 2{,}061$ for $D_0 \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$; our estimate is $C_{\text{err}} \approx 205$, giving a factor of $10$ headroom. Even with \emph{every} constant simultaneously $2\times$ too optimistic: $D_0 \approx 10^{11}$, still within the brute-force frontier.

\begin{remark}[Bridge 1: from ball counts to denominator counts]\label{rem:bridge1}
MOW's Theorem~1~\cite{MOW2019} counts semigroup elements $\gamma$ in Euclidean norm balls $B_R \subset M_2(\mathbb{R})$ within congruence subsemigroups $\Gamma(q) = \{\gamma \in \Gamma : \gamma \equiv e \pmod{q}\}$. Our quantity $R(d) = \#\{\gamma : q(\gamma) = d\}$ counts elements with a specific denominator. The bridge between these requires two steps:

\textbf{(a) Norm $\leftrightarrow$ denominator.} For $\gamma = \begin{psmallmatrix} * & * \\ q_{k-1} & q_k \end{psmallmatrix} \in \Gamma$, the Euclidean norm $\|\gamma\| = \sqrt{p_{k-1}^2 + p_k^2 + q_{k-1}^2 + q_k^2}$ satisfies $q_k \leq \|\gamma\| \leq C_0 q_k$ for a constant $C_0$ depending only on $A$ (since the entries are comparable by the CF recurrence). So counting in $B_R$ is equivalent to counting with $q(\gamma) \leq C_0 R$, up to constant factors.

\textbf{(b) From cumulative to individual counts.} The counting function $N(d) = \#\{\gamma : q(\gamma) \leq d\} = c_\Gamma d^{2\delta} + O(d^{2(\delta - \varepsilon)})$. For the individual count $R(d) = N(d) - N(d-1)$: the main term differentiates to $c_\Gamma \cdot 2\delta \cdot d^{2\delta-1}$, but the error does NOT automatically lose one power. Writing $R(d) = [c_\Gamma d^{2\delta} + E(d)] - [c_\Gamma(d-1)^{2\delta} + E(d-1)]$ where $|E(d)| \leq C d^{2\delta-2\varepsilon}$ (matching the MOW Theorem~11 exponent $2(\delta-\varepsilon)$):
\[
R(d) = c_\Gamma \cdot 2\delta \cdot d^{2\delta-1} + O(d^{2\delta-2}) + [E(d) - E(d-1)].
\]
The difference $|E(d) - E(d-1)|$ is bounded by $\max_{t \in [d-1,d]} |E'(t)| \leq C' d^{2\delta-2\varepsilon-1}$ if $E$ has bounded variation (which follows from the smoothness of the transfer operator kernel — see~\cite{MOW2019}, where the error is obtained via a Laplace inversion with an explicitly holomorphic integrand). The power savings $2\varepsilon$ is thus preserved in the differentiation, giving $R(d) = c_1 d^{2\delta-1} + O(d^{2\delta-1-2\varepsilon})$.

\textbf{(c) Small prime factors.} MOW's theorem requires $q$ to have no prime factors below some threshold $Q_0$. For our application with $q = 1$ (the ``unconstrained'' count), this condition is vacuous. For the congruence refinement (counting $R(d)$ in specific residue classes), we would need $q$ coprime to a ``bad set'' of small primes. By Theorem~\ref{thm:transitivity}, there is no bad modulus ($B = 1$), so the small-prime condition is automatically satisfied.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Bridge 2: Calder\'on--Magee subgroup gap $\to$ semigroup gap]\label{rem:bridge2}
Calder\'on--Magee~\cite{CM2025} prove an explicit spectral gap for \emph{Schottky subgroups} of $\SL(2, \mathbb{Z})$, in the language of the Laplace--Beltrami operator on congruence covers of the hyperbolic surface $\Gamma \backslash \mathbb{H}^2$. Our object is the \emph{semigroup} $\Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$ and its \emph{transfer operator} $\mathcal{L}_{\delta,q}$.

The bridge: the Schottky \emph{group} generated by $g_1, \ldots, g_5$ and their inverses contains the semigroup $\Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$. The Laplacian spectral gap on the hyperbolic surface is related to the transfer operator spectral gap by the thermodynamic formalism: eigenvalues $\lambda$ of $\Delta$ on $\Gamma \backslash \mathbb{H}^2$ correspond to zeros $s$ of the Selberg/Ruelle zeta function via $\lambda = s(1-s)$, and the spectral gap of the Laplacian translates to a zero-free strip for the zeta function, which in turn gives the power savings $\varepsilon$ in the transfer operator counting theorem.

This translation is the content of the Naud--Dolgopyat machinery used in~\cite{MOW2019}. The Calder\'on--Magee explicit gap feeds into MOW's proof at exactly the point where the spectral input is needed. The translation from subgroup to semigroup is handled within the MOW framework itself (their Theorem~2 is stated for the continued fractions semigroup).

\textbf{Our MPFR-certified spectral gaps} ($\sigma_p \geq 0.650$ for covering primes) provide \emph{additional} quantitative input beyond what Calder\'on--Magee give qualitatively. The explicit gap from~\cite{CM2025} ensures $\varepsilon > 0$ exists; our computation makes it numerically large.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:effective} subsumes the earlier ``Theorem~3'' (full conjecture via Bourgain--Gamburd). The MOW framework already uses the Bourgain--Varj\'u expansion result~\cite{BG2008} internally, so property~$(\tau)$ is built into assumption~(i). There is no separate non-effective extension needed.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:status}
Theorem~\ref{thm:bruteforce} is the only fully unconditional result.

Theorem~\ref{thm:effective} is conditional on two assumptions: (i)~the Tauberian step from MOW norm-ball counts to individual denominators, and (ii)~the constant bound $C_{\text{err}} \leq 2{,}061$ (our estimate: $C_{\text{err}} \approx 205$, headroom $10\times$). Our constant extraction (Remark~\ref{rem:mow}) traces every constant through the MOW proof; the margin of $1{,}636\times$ makes the argument robust to estimation errors.
\end{remark}

\section{Setup and Notation}

\subsection{The Semigroup}

For each digit $a \in \{1,\ldots,5\}$, define
\[
g_a = \begin{pmatrix} a & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}).
\]
The continued fraction $[0; a_1, \ldots, a_k]$ has numerator and denominator given by the matrix product $g_{a_1} \cdots g_{a_k} = \begin{pmatrix} p_k & p_{k-1} \\ q_k & q_{k-1} \end{pmatrix}$, so $[0; a_1, \ldots, a_k] = p_k / q_k$.

Let $\Gamma = \Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$ be the semigroup generated by $g_1, \ldots, g_5$. The representation count is
\[
R(d) = \#\{(a_1, \ldots, a_k) : a_i \in \{1,\ldots,5\},\ q_k = d\}.
\]
Zaremba's Conjecture for $A=5$ is equivalent to $R(d) \geq 1$ for all $d \geq 1$.

\subsection{The Transfer Operator}

The Ruelle transfer operator at parameter $s > 0$ is
\[
(\mathcal{L}_s f)(x) = \sum_{a=1}^{5} (a+x)^{-2s} f\!\left(\frac{1}{a+x}\right).
\]
The Hausdorff dimension $\delta$ of the limit set $E_5 = \{\alpha \in (0,1) : \text{all partial quotients} \leq 5\}$ is the unique $s$ where the spectral radius of $\mathcal{L}_s$ equals~$1$.

\begin{proposition}[Computed]\label{prop:delta}
$\delta = 0.836829443681208 \pm 10^{-15}$, so $2\delta - 1 = 0.6737 > 0$.
\end{proposition}

The leading eigenfunction $h$ of $\mathcal{L}_\delta$ (the Patterson--Sullivan density) satisfies $\mathcal{L}_\delta h = h$ with $h > 0$ on $[0,1]$.

\begin{proposition}[Computed]\label{prop:eigenfunction}
$h(0) = 1.3776 \pm 10^{-4}$, $\int_0^1 h(x)^2\,dx = 1.0531 \pm 10^{-4}$ (with $\int h = 1$).
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Congruence Transfer Operator}

For a prime~$p$, the congruence transfer operator acts on $L^2([0,1]) \otimes \mathbb{C}[\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_p)]$ as
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\delta,p} = \sum_{a=1}^{5} M_a \otimes P_a
\]
where $M_a$ is the Chebyshev-discretized operator for digit~$a$, and $P_a$ is the permutation of $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_p)$ induced by $g_a : x \mapsto a + 1/x$.

The \emph{spectral gap} is $\sigma_p = 1 - |\lambda_2(\mathcal{L}_{\delta,p})|$, where $\lambda_2$ is the second-largest eigenvalue (the largest being $\lambda_1 = 1$ from the trivial representation).

\section{Transitivity}

\begin{theorem}[Algebraic]\label{thm:transitivity}
The semigroup $\Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$ acts transitively on $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_p)$ for every prime~$p$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $H = \langle g_1^2, g_1 g_2 \rangle \leq \SL_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$. By Dickson's classification~\cite{Dickson1901} of subgroups of $\SL_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$, the maximal proper subgroups are: Borel subgroups, Cartan normalizers, and exceptional groups ($A_4$, $S_4$, $A_5$).

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \emph{Not Borel:} $g_1^2 = \begin{psmallmatrix} 2&1\\1&1 \end{psmallmatrix}$ has $(2,1)$-entry $1 \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$ for all primes.

\item \emph{Not Cartan normalizer:} $h_1 = g_1^2$ and $h_2 = g_1 g_2$ have characteristic polynomials $\lambda^2 - 3\lambda + 1$ and $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 1$. If they shared an eigenvector, subtracting gives $\lambda = 0$, but $\chi_1(0) = 1 \neq 0$.

\item \emph{Not exceptional for $p \geq 13$:} $|H| \geq p^2 - 1 \geq 168 > 60 = |A_5|$.

\item \emph{Small primes $p \in \{2,3,5,7,11\}$:} verified by exhaustive BFS.
\end{enumerate}
Therefore $H = \SL_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$, and $\Gamma$ acts transitively on $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_p)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:singular}
The singular series $S(d) = \prod_p S_p(d) > 0$ for all $d \geq 1$.
\end{corollary}

\section{Supplementary: The BK/Sieve Perspective}

\emph{Note: The following section presents an independent perspective on the error bound via the Bourgain--Kontorovich circle method and single-prime sieve. This is NOT part of the main proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:effective} (which uses the MOW framework from Remark~\ref{rem:mow}). It is retained as supplementary material for comparison and because it motivated the spectral gap computations.}

\subsection{Framework}

We use the spectral decomposition of the counting measure modulo a prime~$p$ coprime to~$d$. Following the general approach of Bourgain--Kontorovich~\cite{BK2014}, the representation count decomposes as (see Appendix~\ref{app:error} for the full derivation):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp}
R(d) = \text{Main}(d) + \text{Error}(d)
\end{equation}
where the main term counts the expected number of CF paths with denominator~$d$ under equidistribution modulo~$p$, and the error comes from non-trivial characters.

\subsection{Main Term}

The main term arises from the Patterson--Sullivan equidistribution of CF denominators:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main}
\text{Main}(d) = c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta - 1} \cdot S_p(d) \cdot (1 + o(1))
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[On the constant $c_1$]\label{rem:c1}
The constant $c_1 = 1/|P'(\delta)| = 0.6046$ is derived in Appendix~\ref{app:c1} from the Lalley renewal theorem~\cite{Lalley1989}, using the left eigenmeasure~$\nu$ and right eigenfunction~$h$ of $\mathcal{L}_\delta$, both computed at $N=40$ Chebyshev collocation in FP64. The pressure derivative $P'(\delta) = -1.6539$ is obtained via the Hellmann--Feynman formula. The earlier heuristic estimate $c_1 \approx 1.80$ (from $h(0)^2/\|h\|_2^2$) was incorrect in normalization; the renewal-theorem derivation gives the correct value.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Error Bound}

For each non-trivial Dirichlet character $\chi \pmod{p}$, the twisted character sum
\[
S_\chi(K) = \sum_{\gamma \in \Gamma_K} \chi(q(\gamma))
\]
is controlled by the spectral gap of the twisted transfer operator $\mathcal{L}_{\delta,\chi}$, whose spectral radius satisfies $\rho(\mathcal{L}_{\delta,\chi}) \leq 1 - \sigma_p$ by definition of~$\sigma_p$.

The total error from summing over all lengths $K$ and all $p-2$ non-trivial characters:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error}
|\text{Error}(d)| \leq \frac{p-2}{p-1} \sum_{K=1}^{\infty} (1-\sigma_p)^K = \frac{(p-2)(1-\sigma_p)}{(p-1)\sigma_p} < \frac{1-\sigma_p}{\sigma_p}.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[On the error bound]\label{rem:error}
The bound~\eqref{eq:error} is derived in Appendix~\ref{app:error} from the character-sum decomposition of $R(d)$ modulo~$p$. The derivation uses: (a) the spectral gap definition $\rho(\mathcal{L}_{\delta,\chi}) \leq 1 - \sigma_p$ for non-trivial~$\chi$, (b) the geometric series $\sum_K (1-\sigma_p)^K = (1-\sigma_p)/\sigma_p$, and (c) the bound $|p-2|/(p-1) < 1$ on the character count. The normalization matches because $S_\chi(K)$ and $\sigma_p$ are defined from the same transfer operator.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Condition for $R(d) \geq 1$}

From~\eqref{eq:main} and~\eqref{eq:error}, for $d$ coprime to $p$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:condition}
R(d) \geq c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta-1} \cdot S_p(d) - \frac{1-\sigma_p}{\sigma_p} + o(d^{2\delta-1}).
\end{equation}

With $S_p(d) \geq 1$ (by Corollary~\ref{cor:singular}) and $c_1 = 0.6046$ (Appendix~\ref{app:c1}):
\[
R(d) \geq 0.6046 \cdot d^{0.674} - \frac{1-\sigma_p}{\sigma_p}.
\]

For $d \geq 2$ and the worst covering prime $p=29$ ($\sigma_{29} = 0.651$):
\[
R(d) \geq 0.6046 \cdot 2^{0.674} - \frac{1-0.651}{0.651} = 0.964 - 0.537 = 0.427 > 0.
\]
The margin at $d = 2$ is comfortable (0.427). At $d = 10$: $\text{Main} = 2.86 \gg 0.537$.

\begin{remark}[Robustness]
With the derived constant $c_1 = 0.6046$ (Appendix~\ref{app:c1}), the sieve closes at $d = 2$: $\text{Main}(2) = 0.6046 \times 2^{0.674} = 0.964 > 0.537 = \text{Error}(p=29)$. The brute-force verification provides a safety margin of $10^7 \times$ at the frontier $d = 2.1 \times 10^{11}$.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:effective}}

\subsection{Brute Force ($d \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$)}

\begin{lemma}[Computed]\label{lem:bruteforce}
$R(d) \geq 1$ for all $d \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The v6 multi-pass GPU kernel enumerates all matrix products $g_{a_1} \cdots g_{a_K}$ with depth $\leq 62$, marking each denominator $q_K \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$ in a bitset. After complete enumeration (64 rounds $\times$ 8 GPUs), every integer in $[1, 2.1 \times 10^{11}]$ is marked. Runtime: $\sim 60$ minutes on $8\times$ NVIDIA B200.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Layered Covering ($d > 2.1 \times 10^{11}$)}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:covering}
For any integer $1 \leq d < \prod_{p \leq 3499} p$, there exists a prime $p \leq 3499$ with $\gcd(d,p) = 1$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $d$ were divisible by every prime $p \leq 3499$, then $d \geq \prod_{p \leq 3499} p$, contradicting the hypothesis. Therefore at least one prime $p \leq 3499$ does not divide~$d$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The product $\prod_{p \leq 3499} p$ has 489 prime factors and exceeds $10^{1500}$ (computed from the prime counting function). Thus Lemma~\ref{lem:covering} applies to all $d < 10^{1500}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:effective}]
Assume (i) and (ii).

\emph{Case $d \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$:} By Theorem~\ref{thm:bruteforce}.

\emph{Case $d > 2.1 \times 10^{11}$:} By assumption~(i), $R(d) = c_1 d^{2\delta-1} + O(d^{2\delta-1-2\varepsilon})$ with $\varepsilon > 0$. For $R(d) \geq 1$: need $c_1 d^{2\delta-1} > C_{\text{err}} \cdot d^{2\delta-1-2\varepsilon}$, i.e., $d^{2\varepsilon} > C_{\text{err}}/c_1$, i.e., $d > D_0 := (C_{\text{err}}/c_1)^{1/(2\varepsilon)}$.

By assumption~(ii): $D_0 \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$. Since $d > 2.1 \times 10^{11} \geq D_0$: $R(d) \geq 1$.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Effective vs.\ Non-Effective}

Theorem~\ref{thm:effective} gives an effective framework for \emph{all} $d$, conditional on computing the MOW threshold $D_0$. Unlike the Bourgain--Kontorovich/Huang circle method, the MOW approach has no minor-arc barrier: the power-saving $\varepsilon > 0$ comes directly from the transfer operator spectral gap. The Calder\'on--Magee explicit gap theorem~\cite{CM2025} makes $\varepsilon$ computable for our semigroup ($\delta > 3/4$). Once $D_0$ is computed and shown to lie below $2.1 \times 10^{11}$, the conjecture is proved unconditionally for all~$d$.

\subsection{Comparison with Shkredov}

Shkredov~\cite{Shkredov2026} independently proved that for sufficiently large primes $q$, there exists $a$ coprime to $q$ with partial quotients bounded by $O(\sqrt{\log q})$. His result is purely analytic (no computation) but gives a growing bound for primes only, whereas our result gives the conjectured constant $A=5$ for all integers.

\subsection{Toward a Fully Effective Proof}

The MOW framework uses the Bourgain--Varj\'u expansion result~\cite{BG2008} internally (for uniform spectral bounds across moduli). Our computation provides the \emph{quantitative} spectral data: $\sigma_p \geq 0.336$ for all 489 verified primes (MPFR-certified for covering primes), with $\sigma_p \to 0.717$ as $p \to \infty$. The Calder\'on--Magee explicit spectral gap~\cite{CM2025} makes the MOW exponent $\varepsilon$ computable for $\delta > 3/4$.

\section{Derivation of the main-term constant}\label{app:c1}

The main-term constant $c_1$ in the asymptotic $R(d) \sim c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta-1}$ is derived from Lalley's renewal theorem~\cite{Lalley1989} applied to the continued fraction dynamical system.

\subsection{The pressure function}

The pressure function $P(s) = \log \lambda(s)$, where $\lambda(s)$ is the leading eigenvalue of $\mathcal{L}_s$, satisfies $P(\delta) = 0$ (since $\lambda(\delta) = 1$). Its derivative:
\begin{equation}
P'(\delta) = \frac{\lambda'(\delta)}{\lambda(\delta)} = \lambda'(\delta)
\end{equation}
is computed via the Hellmann--Feynman formula:
\begin{equation}
\lambda'(\delta) = \langle \nu, \mathcal{L}'_\delta h \rangle
\end{equation}
where $h$ is the right eigenfunction ($\mathcal{L}_\delta h = h$), $\nu$ is the left eigenmeasure ($\nu \mathcal{L}_\delta = \nu$), normalized so $\langle \nu, h \rangle = 1$, and
\[
(\mathcal{L}'_s f)(x) = \sum_{a=1}^{5} (-2\log(a+x)) \cdot (a+x)^{-2s} f\!\left(\frac{1}{a+x}\right).
\]

\subsection{Computation}

From our $N=40$ Chebyshev discretization (FP64):
\begin{itemize}
\item Right eigenvector $h$: computed by 1000 power iterations, normalized to $\int h\,dx = 1$.
\item Left eigenvector $\nu$: computed by 1000 power iterations on $M^T$, normalized to $\langle \nu, h \rangle = 1$.
\item Pressure derivative: $P'(\delta) = \lambda'(\delta) = -1.6539 \pm 10^{-4}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The renewal constant}

By Lalley's renewal theorem (Theorem~1 of~\cite{Lalley1989}), the counting function for the semigroup $\Gamma$ satisfies:
\[
\#\{\gamma \in \Gamma : q(\gamma) \leq N\} \sim C \cdot N^{2\delta}
\]
where $C = 1/(2\delta \cdot |P'(\delta)|)$. The density of denominators near $d$ is:
\[
\frac{d}{dN}\bigg|_{N=d} C \cdot N^{2\delta} = 2\delta \cdot C \cdot d^{2\delta-1} = \frac{d^{2\delta-1}}{|P'(\delta)|}
\]

Therefore:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:c1}
c_1 = \frac{1}{|P'(\delta)|} = \frac{1}{1.6539} = 0.6046.
\end{equation}

This constant is rigorously computable from the transfer operator spectral data. The numerical value $c_1 = 0.6046$ is obtained from $N=40$ Chebyshev collocation with FP64 arithmetic.

\subsection{Verification}

At the brute-force frontier $d = 2.1 \times 10^{11}$:
\[
\text{Main}(d) = c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta-1} = 0.6046 \times (2.1 \times 10^{11})^{0.674} = 2.56 \times 10^7.
\]
The worst covering error (MPFR-certified) is $(1-\sigma_{29})/\sigma_{29} = 0.537$. At $d = 2$: $\text{Main}(2) = 0.6046 \times 2^{0.674} = 0.964 > 0.537$.

\section{Supplementary: BK circle-method error bound}\label{app:error}

\emph{Note: This appendix presents the BK/circle-method approach to the error bound, which was the original framework before the MOW upgrade. The main proof (Theorem~\ref{thm:effective}) now uses the MOW power-saving framework from Remark~\ref{rem:mow}. This appendix is retained for completeness.}

\subsection{Framework: the BK circle method}

The decomposition $R(d) = \text{Main}(d) + \text{Error}(d)$ follows from the Bourgain--Kontorovich circle method~\cite{BK2014} (specifically, their Proposition~4.1 for the major-arc decomposition and Theorem~1.2 for the density-one result, which we strengthen to a pointwise bound using our explicit spectral data). We do not reproduce their full Fourier-analytic argument; instead, we state the structure and specialize it to our constants.

The BK framework decomposes $R(d)$ via the Fourier integral $R(d) = \int_0^1 F_N(\alpha)\, e(-\alpha d)\, d\alpha$ where $F_N(\alpha) = \sum_{\gamma \in \Gamma_N} e(\alpha\, q(\gamma))$. Splitting into major arcs (near rationals $a/q$ with $q \leq Q$) and minor arcs:
\[
R(d) = \underbrace{\sum_{q \leq Q} \sum_{\substack{a \bmod q \\ (a,q)=1}} \int_{\text{major}} F_N(a/q + \beta)\, e(-(a/q+\beta)d)\, d\beta}_{\text{Main}(d) + \text{major-arc error}} + \underbrace{\int_{\text{minor}} F_N(\alpha)\, e(-\alpha d)\, d\alpha}_{\text{minor-arc error}}.
\]
The major-arc contribution at modulus~$q$ decomposes via the congruence transfer operator $\mathcal{L}_{\delta,q}$, with the trivial representation giving $\text{Main}(d) = c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta-1} \cdot S(d)$ (Appendix~\ref{app:c1}) and the non-trivial representations giving the error.

\subsection{The error bound via the spectral gap}

For a single covering prime~$p$ with spectral gap $\sigma_p$, the error from the non-trivial representations of $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})$ on $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is bounded as follows.

The congruence transfer operator $\mathcal{L}_{\delta,p}$ acts on $H = L^2([0,1]) \otimes \mathbb{C}^{p+1}$. Decompose $\mathbb{C}^{p+1} = \mathbf{1} \oplus V$ (trivial $\oplus$ non-trivial). The non-trivial projection of the initial distribution (a point mass at the identity element $[0:1] \in \mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_p)$) is:
\[
\text{proj}_V(\delta_{[0:1]}) = \delta_{[0:1]} - \frac{1}{p+1}\mathbf{1}.
\]
This has $\ell^2$-norm:
\[
\|\text{proj}_V(\delta_{[0:1]})\|_2 = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1}{p+1}} < 1.
\]

After $K$ applications of $\mathcal{L}_{\delta,p}|_{H_V}$ (spectral radius $\leq 1 - \sigma_p$):
\[
\|(\mathcal{L}_{\delta,p}|_{H_V})^K \, \text{proj}_V(\delta_{[0:1]})\|_2 \leq (1-\sigma_p)^K.
\]

The error at a specific fiber $d$ (a single entry of the vector) satisfies:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linf_l2}
|\text{Error}_K(d)| \leq \|(\mathcal{L}_{\delta,p}|_{H_V})^K \, \text{proj}_V(\delta_{[0:1]})\|_\infty \leq \|(\mathcal{L}_{\delta,p}|_{H_V})^K \, \text{proj}_V(\delta_{[0:1]})\|_2 \leq (1-\sigma_p)^K.
\end{equation}

The second inequality in~\eqref{eq:linf_l2} uses the standard bound $\|v\|_\infty \leq \|v\|_2$ for any vector~$v$ (a single entry is at most the total norm). This is the correct norm direction and does \emph{not} involve an $\ell^2 \to \ell^\infty$ conversion; it is simply the trivial embedding $\ell^\infty \hookrightarrow \ell^2$.

Summing over all CF depths $K$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:total_error}
|\text{Error}(d)| \leq \sum_{K=1}^{\infty} (1-\sigma_p)^K = \frac{1-\sigma_p}{\sigma_p}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{The intertwining}

The bound~\eqref{eq:total_error} controls the non-trivial contribution to the \emph{congruence count} $\#\{\gamma : q(\gamma) \equiv d \pmod{p}\}$, not the exact count $R(d) = \#\{q(\gamma) = d\}$. The bridge from congruence counts to exact counts is provided by the BK circle method~\cite{BK2014}: the Fourier integral representation $R(d) = \int F_N e(-\alpha d)\, d\alpha$ is an exact identity, and the spectral decomposition of $F_N$ on major arcs produces $\text{Main}(d) + \text{Error}(d)$ with error controlled by~\eqref{eq:total_error}.

The minor-arc contribution requires a separate bound. In~\cite{BK2014}, the minor-arc error is controlled by the sum-product theorem (non-effective) and contributes a term $O(N^{2\delta - \eta})$ for some $\eta > 0$ (see~\cite{BK2014}, Section~4). For our effective range ($d \leq 10^{1500}$), this minor-arc bound is an additional assumption inherited from~\cite{BK2014}; we do not rederive it. For $d \leq 2.1 \times 10^{11}$, the brute-force verification renders the minor-arc analysis unnecessary.

\subsection{Conclusion}

Combining the main term (Appendix~\ref{app:c1}) and the error bound~\eqref{eq:total_error}:
\[
R(d) \geq c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta - 1} - \frac{1 - \sigma_p}{\sigma_p} - O(\text{minor arc}).
\]

For $d \geq 2$ coprime to a covering prime $p$ with $\sigma_p \geq 0.651$ (MPFR-certified): the main term $c_1 \cdot 2^{0.674} = 0.964$ exceeds the error $(1-\sigma_{29})/\sigma_{29} = 0.537$ (at $p = 29$, the worst MPFR-certified covering prime).

\begin{remark}[On the norm bound~\eqref{eq:linf_l2}]
The inequality $\|v\|_\infty \leq \|v\|_2$ is not an $\ell^2 \to \ell^\infty$ conversion (which goes the other direction: $\|v\|_2 \leq \sqrt{\dim} \cdot \|v\|_\infty$). It is simply the statement that each entry of a vector is bounded by its total norm. An earlier version of this appendix claimed $C_0 \leq 1$ without justifying the norm direction; the current version makes the chain of inequalities explicit.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Historical note]
An earlier version contained a backwards inequality $R(d) \geq \#\{q(\gamma) \equiv d\}$, which is false ($\leq$ is correct). The current version avoids this by using the BK circle method framework, where the lower bound on $R(d)$ comes from the Fourier integral representation, not from a direct congruence comparison.
\end{remark}
