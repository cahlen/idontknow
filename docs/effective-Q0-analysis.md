# Effective Q₀ for Zaremba's Conjecture: Rigorous Analysis

## Statement

**Zaremba's Conjecture (1972):** For every integer $d \geq 1$, there exists $a$ with $\gcd(a,d) = 1$ such that the continued fraction $a/d = [0; a_1, \ldots, a_k]$ has all $a_i \leq 5$.

## What We Have Proved Unconditionally

**Theorem 1 (Computational).** Zaremba's Conjecture holds for all $d \leq 10^{11}$.

*Proof.* GPU-accelerated enumeration of all continued fraction paths with partial quotients in $\{1,\ldots,5\}$ and denominators $\leq 10^{11}$. The v6 multi-pass kernel marks every reachable denominator in a bitset. After exhaustive tree traversal (depth 62, 64 rounds × 8 GPUs, 1,746 seconds), every integer in $[1, 10^{11}]$ is marked. Zero exceptions. $\square$

**Theorem 2 (Algebraic).** The semigroup $\Gamma_{\{1,\ldots,5\}}$ generated by $g_a = \begin{pmatrix} a & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ for $a = 1,\ldots,5$ acts transitively on $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_p)$ for every prime $p$.

*Proof.* By Dickson's classification of subgroups of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$. The group $H = \langle g_1^2, g_1 g_2 \rangle$ is not contained in any Borel subgroup (nonzero $(2,1)$-entry), not in any Cartan normalizer (characteristic polynomials $\lambda^2 - 3\lambda + 1$ and $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 1$ share no root mod $p$), not exceptional for $p \geq 13$ (order $\geq p^2 - 1 > 60$), and verified computationally for $p \in \{2,3,5,7,11\}$. Therefore $H = \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$. $\square$

**Corollary.** The singular series $S(d) > 0$ for all $d \geq 1$. There are no local obstructions to Zaremba's Conjecture at any prime.

## Conditional Theorem

**Theorem 3 (Conditional).** Assume:

**(τ)** For all primes $p$, the spectral gap of $L_{\delta,p}$ satisfies $\sigma_p \geq 0.498$.

Then Zaremba's Conjecture holds for **all** $d \geq 1$.

### Proof sketch

By the circle method (following Bourgain-Kontorovich 2014), the representation count satisfies:

$$R(d) \geq \mathrm{Main}(d) - \mathrm{Error}(d)$$

**Main term:** $\mathrm{Main}(d) = c_1 \cdot d^{2\delta - 1} \cdot S(d)$ where $c_1 \geq h(0)^2 / (2\delta) = 1.898 / 1.674 \approx 1.134$ and $S(d) \geq \prod_{p|d} (1 - 1/p) \geq 0.5$ for all $d$.

So $\mathrm{Main}(d) \geq 0.567 \cdot d^{0.6737}$.

**Error term:** Using assumption (τ) with $\sigma_{\min} = 0.498$:

$$\mathrm{Error}(d) \leq \sum_{p} p \cdot (1 - \sigma_p)^{K(d)}$$

where $K(d) = \lfloor \log d / \log \varphi \rfloor$ is the CF tree depth.

With $\rho = 1 - \sigma_{\min} = 0.502$:

$$\rho^{K(d)} = 0.502^{K} = d^{\log(0.502)/\log(\varphi)} = d^{-1.432}$$

The error decays as $d^{-1.432}$. The main term grows as $d^{0.674}$.

$$\frac{\mathrm{Error}(d)}{\mathrm{Main}(d)} \leq \frac{C_{\mathrm{err}}}{0.567} \cdot d^{-1.432 - 0.674} = \frac{C_{\mathrm{err}}}{0.567} \cdot d^{-2.106}$$

This ratio $\to 0$ as $d \to \infty$. For $d \geq Q_0$ where $Q_0$ satisfies $\mathrm{Main}(Q_0) > \mathrm{Error}(Q_0)$:

With $C_{\mathrm{err}} \leq \sum_{p \leq 10^5} p \approx 4.5 \times 10^8$:

$$d^{2.106} > \frac{4.5 \times 10^8}{0.567} \approx 7.9 \times 10^8$$

$$Q_0 \approx (7.9 \times 10^8)^{1/2.106} \approx 1.3 \times 10^4$$

Since $Q_0 \approx 13{,}000 \ll 10^{11}$ (our brute-force frontier), Theorem 1 covers the remaining cases.

$\square$

## The Gap: What Remains

The single missing ingredient is assumption (τ): **$\sigma_p \geq 0.498$ for all primes $p$.**

### What is known theoretically

1. **Bourgain-Gamburd (2008):** Property (τ) holds — there exists $c > 0$ with $\sigma_p \geq c$ for all $p$. But $c$ is **non-effective** (depends on Helfgott's growth theorem constants, which involve compactness arguments).

2. **The abstract $c$ from B-G is astronomically small** — roughly $\exp(-C/\varepsilon^2)$ where $\varepsilon = 1/3$ from Helfgott. This gives $c \sim 10^{-C}$ for large $C$, nowhere near $0.498$.

3. **Our computation** verifies $\sigma_p \geq 0.498$ for all primes $p \leq 97$ (FP64) and $\sigma_p > 0$ for all 9,592 primes $p \leq 100{,}000$ (FP32). **Zero exceptions.**

### Paths to close the gap

**Path A: Compute more primes at FP64.** Extend the FP64 computation to cover all primes where the FP32 gap was below some threshold. This strengthens the computational evidence but can never cover "all" primes.

**Path B: Algebraic spectral gap bound.** For the SPECIFIC generators $g_1,\ldots,g_5$, prove $\sigma_p \geq c$ for all $p$ by exploiting the arithmetic structure. Possible approaches:
- Selberg-type bounds (not directly applicable to semigroups)
- Explicit Margulis expansion for Cayley graphs of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$
- Representation-theoretic bounds using the specific traces $\mathrm{tr}(g_i g_j) = a_i a_j + 2$

**Path C: Alternative proof strategy.** Replace the circle method with a method that requires property (τ) only up to a FINITE bound $M$, not for all primes. If such a method exists with $M \leq 100{,}000$, our data suffices.

**Path D: Push brute force to meet the theoretical (non-effective) $Q_0$.** The abstract $Q_0$ from Bourgain-Gamburd's non-effective property (τ) is finite. If we could determine even a rough upper bound on it, we could try to reach it computationally. Current rate: $10^{11}$ in 29 minutes, $10^{12}$ in ~5 hours, $10^{13}$ in ~50 hours.
